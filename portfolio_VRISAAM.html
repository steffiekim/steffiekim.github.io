VR Research Project

This is a quantitative research project I conducted as one of the 3 studies for my doctoral dissertation at USC

Project

Understanding Barriers and Facilitators to Adopting VR Interventions

This study investigated elements that can either facilitate or block potential users from using VR as an intervention to treat social anxiety symptoms.

Photo created by DCStudio at Freepik

The Problem

While VR is becoming more widespread with affordable consumer VR devices, it is still not considered a universal medium among the public for daily use. Similarly, VR use in treating mental health disorders is still in its “pioneering” stage. Therefore, understanding barriers to VR intervention among individuals with SAD (iSAD) is key in achieving a broader reach and rapid dissemination.

Research Questions

What are the barriers and facilitators to adopting VR interventions for treating social anxiety?

The Goal

  • Invesitgate barriers and facilitators to the adoption of VR intervention for iSAD.
  • Produce a comprehensive model—Virtual Reality Intervention for Social Anxiety Acceptance Model (VRISAAM)—including all the barriers and facilitators


    • Research Design

  • 387 iSAD were recruited on Prolific in June 2022.
  • After screening attention checks and knowledge test on a reading material about what VR intervention is, a total of 269 iSAD were analyzed.
  • Survey items were either adopted from the Technology Acceptance Model 3 (Venkatesh & Davis, 1996) or created as needed for establishing VRISAAM.


    • Analysis

  • Path analysis was conducted in R using the lavaan package.

    • Figure 1. Results of path analysis


    Key Findings

    Barriers to adopting VR intervention for iSAD:
  • VR Sickness leads to decreased perceived ease of use and decreased intention to use VR interventions.

    • Favilitators to adopting VR intervention for iSAD:
  • Subjective norm and perceived usefulness positively predict intention to use VR interventions.
  • Subjective norm positively predicts perceived usefuness and perceived ease of use.
  • VR efficacy, trust toward therapy, and VR sickness positively predict perceived ease of use.
  • Trust toward therapy positively predicts perceived usefulness.
