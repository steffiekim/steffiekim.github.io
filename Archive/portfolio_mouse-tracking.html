Project #3

#3. Mouse-Tracking Social Decisions
in Conflicting Situations (In Progress)

This is an ongoing sole-authored research project as a part of my doctoral dissertation. The high-level objective of the study is to investigate how differently framed messages can nudge people to make socially-approaching decisions by tracking their live mouse movements.

The Problem

Individuals with social anxiety disorder (iSAD) often experience two conflicting motives in social situations: a) wanting to socialize and b) liking alone time. Little research has been conducted to understand whether a gain-framed message or a loss-framed message nudges iSAD to make a decision to either approach or avoid social situations in the moment of decision-making. Thus, this study addresses this problem by live-tracking mouse movements when iSAD make social decisions.

The Goal

Conduct quantitative research to identify (a) the message framing that nudges iSAD to approach social situations and (b) the patterns of hesitations in the moment of social decision-making.

Research Questions

1. Which message framing (gain vs. loss) is more likely to nudge iSAD to approach social situations?

2. What are the patterns of mouse movements when unconscious hesitations are triggered in decision-making?

Research Design

• An online experiment was conducted in June 2021
• A total of 200 participants will be analyzed

Eligibility Criteria
• 18 years and older
• Have a clinical level of social anxiety disorder based on the DSM-5 diagnostic criteria
• Score more than the cutoff value of 30 (out of 144) in the Liebowitz Social Anxiety Scale

Experimental Design
• 2 (message framing: gain vs. loss) X 2 (motivational orientation: approach vs. avoidance; see figures below) between-subjects design

Analysis Plan

• All data will be analyzed in R

Mouse-Tracking Data
• The raw data files will be preprocessed to align all mouse trajectories to the same initial start coordinates
• The data will be time-normalized such that each trajectory contains the same number of recorded coordinates (i.e., 101 equally spaced time steps) regardless of the response time
