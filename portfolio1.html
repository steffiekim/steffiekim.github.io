Project #1

#1. Communication Strategies of
Socially Anxious Individuals

This is a formative research project I led as a first author. The high-level objective of the study is to understand the psychological model of individuals with social anxiety disorder in order to develop an efficacious intervention for those who need communication skills.

The Problem

Individuals with social anxiety disorder (iSAD) experience adversity in daily life due to the disorder (e.g., lower educational and work achievement compared to their healthy counterparts). Thus, they are prone to social isolation, even though they desire intimate interpersonal relationships. Yet, little research is devoted to understanding in detail when they experience social anxiety, what factors determine their social anxiety, and how they handle it. Thus, it is difficult to provide optimized and realistic treatment for iSAD.

The Goal

Conduct formative research to discover (a) daily social situations in which iSAD experience social anxiety and (b) communication patterns they use to overcome social anxiety.

Research Questions

1. When do iSAD experience social anxiety?

2. What factors determine iSAD's social anxiety?

3. What are iSAD's communication strategies to manage social anxiety?

Research Design

Recruited and interviewed 27 individuals with social anxiety disorder (about 1 hour for each participant)

Eligibility Criteria
• Between the ages of 18 and 22 (M = 20.41, SD = 2.95)
• Have a clinical level of social anxiety disorder based on the DSM-5 diagnostic criteria
• Scores more than the cutoff value of 30 (out of 144) in the Liebowitz Social Anxiety Scale

Analysis

• Transcribed 27 Interviews • Employed the 6 phases of thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006) using NVivo

Key Findings

iSAD experience social anxiety when:
• Their goals and the other party's goals are different
• There is a mismatch in the common background (e.g., from a different city)
• Their self-image is perceived differently (both positively and negatively) than their expectation

Factors that determine the level of social anxiety:
• Benefits of socializing (e.g., What makes socializing better than staying at home?)
• Communication competency (e.g., Can I present myself as a fun person?)
• The amiability of the other party (e.g., Is this person friendly enough?)

iSAD's communication strategies to manage social anxiety:
• Avoid socializing actively (e.g., do not attend the event) or passively (e.g., go on the phone at the party)
• Pursue social interactions when inevitable, but plan ahead or collect information about other people
• Put on a different persona (e.g., assuming a character from a TV show)

Research Impact

Lays a foundation for future interventions targeting iSAD
• Clinicians could train iSAD to practice how to respond to a range of typical scripted social situations while habituating them to the social complexities of these contexts
• Interventionists can train iSAD by initiating social interactions with friendly partners at the beginning stage and gradually decreasing the conversation partner’s level of affability over time
• iSAD could be trained in identifying and responding to perceived goal differences and achieving mutually rewarding social interactions
• The illustrated situations can be designed as virtual reality (VR) interventions, where participants can experience social interactions in safe (i.e., realistic but not actual) environments

Provides a psychological model of iSAD
• iSAD have ambivalent feelings about socializing (i.e., the need to belong vs. dislike toward social interactions)
• It is not that iSAD do not want socializing. They desire it but do not have the right social skills
• What we take for granted (e.g., basic social communication skills) may not be naturally learned for iSAD

Achievements

• This research project was published in Health Communication

• The findings of this research project were presented at the 70th International Communication Association conference

• This research project was funded by the Annenberg Summer Research Fellowship ($25K) in 2019
